Skateboarding Filmmakers And Casts

Explore each filmer's favorite cast of skaters

Jared Wilber

Although they live behind the scenes, filmers play a very important role in skateboarding. By mapping their vision behind the lense into the video before I eyes, they shape their own personal brand, as well as define the aesthetic of the skaters and brands they represent.
This article won't go into any big theories about the role of filmmakers in skateboarding (not that size matters!). Instead, it serves as a piece to explore some of the filmers.

There is no filmmaking in skateboarding without skateboarders. I guess this is obvious. Probably did not warrant the first sentence of this paragraph. But here we are. Filmer Favorites

From the graphs above, it's clear that many filmermakers have a favorite 'cast' of favorites they film. Sometimes this is a result of the filmers preferring to film their friends. Other times, this is because the filmers are emlpoyed by a company with a fairly standard cast of skaters.

In any case, I've always wondered, which filmers are the most have the most consistent cast of skaters in their videos? To answer the question, below I've ranked the filmers by what I call the uniformity index: calculated by dividing the total number of parts a filmer has filmed by the unique number of skaters they've filmed. A value of one implies that no skaters were filmed more than once, while higher values indicate a consistent cast of skaters filmed across multiple video-parts. a broader range of skaters .

Filmer Similarity

There are many ways to idenfity similar filmers. The style of their filming. The cameras they use. Keeping in line with the theme of this article, we'll investigate similarity as a metric of who the filmmakers include in their videos. Which filmers are the most similar, with respect to whom they film? To find out, we'll tweak some common information retreival techniques to our unique use case. In particular, we'll use TF-IDF (or, for our case, SF-IFF: skater-frequency inverse filmer frequency): from the global set of skaters across all videos, we'll represent each filmer as the vector of skaters they film, weighted by the number of times they filmed them, as well as the number of times they were filmed by other filmers. Next, we'll use dimensionality reduction techniques to map this to a two-dimensional space, so that we may more easily visualize the data (e.g. in the scatter plot below). As each filmer is now embedded as a representation of the skaters they film, the distance between the filmers represents a meaningful measure of similarity. Data & Methodology

The data was collected from (the now basically defunct) skatevideosite.com. The data comprises videos up until July 2019. As the data in skatevideosite.com is user-entered, we can't guarantee total accuracy. For this reason, some of our mapping from filmmakers to skaters may not be perfect (corrections were made where identified). Additionally, not all filmmakers featued in the dataset were included - lesser-known filmers were excluded, as were filmers with less than four unique videos in dataset.
